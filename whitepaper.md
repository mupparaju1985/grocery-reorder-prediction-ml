# Predicting Customer Reorder Behavior Using Machine Learning

## Business Problem
Customer retention is one of the most significant drivers of long-term profitability in retail and e-commerce environments. While organizations invest heavily in customer acquisition, sustainable growth depends mainly on repeat purchasing behavior. Identifying customers unlikely to reorder products enables businesses to intervene with targeted marketing, personalized promotions, and loyalty initiatives. The objective of this project is to determine whether customer reorder behavior can be accurately predicted using historical transactional and behavioral data. By developing a reliable classification model, organizations can improve retention efforts, allocate marketing resources more effectively, and enhance customer lifetime value.
Background and History
Predictive analytics has become central to modern retail strategy. Early classification models, such as Logistic Regression, provided interpretable and statistically grounded approaches for binary outcome prediction. As data availability and computational capabilities expanded, ensemble learning techniques such as Random Forest emerged as powerful alternatives capable of capturing nonlinear relationships and complex feature interactions.
Customer reorder prediction aligns with established research in behavioral analytics, which holds that prior purchasing patterns are strong indicators of future behavior. Behavioral metrics such as reorder rate, purchase frequency, and recency measures provide quantifiable predictors that can be incorporated into supervised machine learning models. This project follows an end-to-end data science methodology, including exploratory data analysis, feature preparation, model training, evaluation, and interpretation of business implications.
## Data Explanation
The dataset used in this study consists of historical customer transaction records containing product-level purchase information and associated behavioral features. The dependent variable is a binary indicator representing whether a product was reordered. Independent variables include reorder rate, days since prior order, purchase frequency, and additional aggregated behavioral measures derived from customer transaction history.
Data preparation included removing duplicate records, validating data integrity, assessing missing values, and preparing features for modeling. Feature scaling was applied where appropriate to meet the assumptions of Logistic Regression. The dataset was partitioned into training and testing subsets to ensure unbiased performance evaluation.
Exploratory analysis first examined the distribution of reordered versus non-reordered products to assess potential class imbalance. The visualization titled “Distribution of Reordered vs Non-Reordered Products” illustrates the relative proportions of each class. Understanding this distribution is essential because imbalanced datasets can distort accuracy metrics and require careful interpretation of precision and recall.
 

Behavioral timing patterns were then evaluated by analyzing the distribution of days since the prior order. The figure titled “Distribution of Days Since Prior Order” demonstrates purchasing interval trends and provides insight into customer recency behavior. Recency is often a strong predictor of repeat purchase likelihood, and its distribution supports inclusion as a meaningful feature in predictive modeling.
 
Correlation analysis was conducted to evaluate relationships among predictor variables and identify potential multicollinearity concerns. The “Feature Correlation Matrix” visualization illustrates the strength and direction of associations among behavioral variables. The matrix confirms that reorder rate exhibits a strong positive relationship with the target variable, validating its importance as a key predictor.

 
## Methods
Two supervised classification models were developed and compared in this analysis. Logistic Regression was selected due to its interpretability and ability to generate probability estimates for binary outcomes. Random Forest was implemented as an ensemble learning method capable of capturing nonlinear patterns and reducing variance through aggregation of multiple decision trees.
Both models were trained using identical training and testing splits to ensure fair comparison. The performance evaluation included accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, confusion matrix analysis, and Receiver Operating Characteristic curve analysis. The Area Under the ROC Curve was used as a primary metric to evaluate each model’s ability to discriminate between reordered and non-reordered outcomes across different classification thresholds.
## Analysis
Both models demonstrated meaningful predictive capability; however, Random Forest consistently outperformed Logistic Regression across evaluation metrics. The confusion matrix titled “Confusion Matrix – Random Forest” illustrates the model’s classification performance in terms of true positives, true negatives, false positives, and false negatives. The matrix indicates that the model effectively identifies reorder events while maintaining controlled misclassification rates.

 
Receiver Operating Characteristic analysis further evaluated classification performance across varying threshold values. The figure titled “ROC Curve Comparison” compares Logistic Regression and Random Forest performance. The ROC curve shows that Random Forest achieves a higher Area Under the Curve, indicating stronger discrimination and improved ability to distinguish between reordered and non-reordered products.
 
Feature importance analysis revealed that reorder rate is the most influential predictor variable in the Random Forest model. This finding aligns with established behavioral theory, which suggests that historical purchase repetition strongly predicts future buying behavior.
## Conclusion
The findings of this study demonstrate that machine learning techniques can effectively predict customer reorder behavior using transactional and behavioral data. While Logistic Regression provides interpretability and probabilistic insight, Random Forest exhibits superior classification accuracy and stronger discrimination performance. These results support the adoption of ensemble learning methods for predictive retail analytics. Integrating this model into operational systems could enhance targeted marketing strategies, improve customer retention, and optimize business decision-making.
## Assumptions
This analysis assumes that historical purchasing behavior is representative of future patterns. It assumes that the dataset accurately reflects customer activity without systematic bias and that the training and testing subsets are representative of the broader customer population. Additionally, it assumes that behavioral predictors remain relatively stable over time.
## Limitations
The dataset does not incorporate external factors such as seasonality, economic conditions, competitor activity, or pricing fluctuations that may influence reorder behavior. Model performance may decline if consumer purchasing patterns change over time. Continuous monitoring and periodic retraining are necessary to maintain predictive accuracy.
## Challenges
One of the primary challenges encountered during modeling was mitigating the potential for overfitting caused by highly predictive features, such as reorder rate. Ensuring generalization required careful validation and evaluation across multiple performance metrics. Another challenge involved balancing predictive accuracy with interpretability, as ensemble methods provide improved performance but reduced transparency compared to traditional statistical models.
## Future Uses and Additional Applications
The predictive modeling framework developed in this study can be extended to additional applications such as customer churn prediction, personalized recommendation systems, and demand forecasting. Future research may evaluate advanced ensemble techniques or deep learning models to assess whether additional performance improvements can be achieved.
## Recommendations
It is recommended that the Random Forest model be integrated into the organization’s analytics infrastructure to generate reorder probability scores for active customers. These predictions can guide targeted retention campaigns aimed at customers with lower predicted reorder likelihood. Performance metrics such as ROC-AUC and precision-recall should be monitored regularly to detect model drift and ensure sustained effectiveness.
## Implementation Plan
Implementation would require integrating the trained model into the company’s data processing pipeline. Transactional data would be processed in batch or real-time environments, and predicted probabilities would be stored within a business intelligence system accessible to marketing and operations teams. A governance framework should be established to oversee model updates, monitoring procedures, and compliance with privacy regulations.
## Ethical Assessment
The use of customer behavioral data for predictive modeling raises ethical considerations regarding privacy, transparency, and fairness. Customer information must be anonymized and securely stored to prevent unauthorized access. Predictive insights should be used to enhance customer experience rather than manipulate purchasing behavior. Organizations must ensure that automated decision-making systems do not unintentionally disadvantage specific customer segments. Responsible data governance practices are essential to maintaining trust and ethical integrity.
